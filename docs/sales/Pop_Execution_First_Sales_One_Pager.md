# Pop Execution-First Sales One-Pager

## Purpose
This document is designed for a founder with **zero formal sales experience**.  
It gives you a simple way to start outreach, run first conversations, and close a tiny pilot without sounding like a polished salesperson.

You do **not** need to “sell” in a slick way.  
You only need to:
1. contact the right kinds of brands,
2. use a short script,
3. ask for a tiny test,
4. follow up consistently.

---

## What Pop is
Pop is a **pay-for-performance grocery conversion channel** for CPG brands.

When a shopper adds a product to a grocery list, Pop can show a competing brand offer with a rebate.

The brand only pays when the shopper buys and submits a receipt.

That means the pitch is not:
- “buy ads from us”
- “sponsor our platform”
- “run a broad awareness campaign”

The pitch is:

> “We help challenger CPG brands win shoppers away from incumbents at the moment of purchase intent. You only pay for receipt-verified conversion.”

---

## The easiest way to think about the offer
You are **not** asking a brand to believe in a giant future vision.

You are asking them to try a very small experiment:

- small upfront pilot budget, such as **$500**
- example shopper rebate: **$1.50**
- Pop platform fee: **$0.50**
- total cost to brand: **$2.00 per verified conversion**

This makes the decision easier because the brand is not committing to a large contract.

---

## Who to target first
Only go after brands that are likely to say yes to a small experiment.

### Ideal first targets
Look for brands that are:
- challenger brands, not giant incumbents
- already sold in grocery or mass retail
- digitally savvy
- already spending on growth
- likely to care about stealing market share
- small enough to move quickly

### Good categories
- snacks
- beverages
- frozen foods
- better-for-you alternatives
- sauces / condiments
- household products with obvious substitutes

### Bad early targets
Avoid:
- giant legacy brands
- highly regulated products
- brands with very low margins
- brands that require long procurement cycles
- companies where nobody seems to own growth

---

## Who to contact
Start with these titles:

1. Head of Growth  
2. VP of Growth  
3. Director of Shopper Marketing  
4. VP of Ecommerce  
5. Performance Marketing Lead  
6. Brand Manager at a smaller company

If you cannot find the perfect title, just pick the person who seems closest to revenue, ecommerce, or growth.

Would you be open to a quick 15-minute call if I send a 60-second overview?

— Lance

## What to do if they reply
If they respond positively, do **not** over-explain.

Reply with:

> Thanks — happy to keep it simple. The idea is a small pilot where we promote your brand as a swap option when shoppers add a competing item to their grocery list. You only pay on receipt-verified purchase.  
>  
> If helpful, I can walk you through a 15-minute pilot concept and expected cost per verified conversion.

That is all you need.

## The call flow
Your first call does not need to be fancy.

Just follow this structure.

### 1. Intro
“Thanks for taking the time. I’ll keep this short.”

### 2. Simple problem statement
“Most CPG spend pays for impressions, clicks, or broad exposure. We’re trying to create a channel where brands pay only when a shopper actually buys and submits a receipt.”

### 3. Explain Pop in plain English
“When someone adds a competing product to their grocery list, Pop can show your brand as an alternative with a rebate.”

### 4. Why it matters
“The big idea is reaching shoppers when they are showing live purchase intent, not after the fact.”

### 5. Offer the pilot
“We’d start with a tiny pilot — something like a $500 test budget — to see whether we can drive verified trial at a reasonable cost.”

### 6. Ask the easiest question
“Would something like that be worth exploring?”

Then stop talking.

---

## If they ask what makes Pop different
Say this:

> “The difference is timing and accountability. We are trying to influence the shopper when purchase intent is being expressed, and we only charge on receipt-verified purchase.”

If they mention Ibotta, Fetch, Instacart, or retail media, say:

> “Those are useful comparisons, but our angle is intercepting intent at list-build time instead of just showing broad offers or ads.”

---

## If they ask for metrics
Do not overcomplicate it.

Tell them the pilot will measure:
- offer views
- swap accepts
- verified purchases
- cost per verified conversion
- redemption rate

Optional later metrics:
- repeat purchase
- retention
- lift by category

---

## If they ask, “How big is this now?”
Be honest.

Use this:

> “We’re early. Right now we’re looking for a few smart pilot partners so we can prove conversion economics before scaling distribution.”

That answer is good enough.

---

## If they ask for pricing
Use this:

> “For the pilot, I’d keep it very simple: a small prepaid test budget and a fixed cost per verified conversion. The goal is to prove whether this is a repeatable acquisition channel for you.”

If they want numbers, give the example:
- $500 pilot budget
- $2.00 total cost per verified conversion
- shopper rebate plus Pop fee bundled into that

---

## If they say “not now”
Reply with:

> Totally understand. We’re very early and are just looking for a few brands willing to test a measurable conversion channel. If timing changes, I’d love to reconnect.

Then follow up later.

## Weekly execution plan
This is the part you can actually do even with no sales background.

### Every week
1. find 20 target brands  
2. identify 1–2 contacts per brand  
3. send 20–40 short outreach messages  
4. track responses in a simple spreadsheet  
5. book any interested calls immediately  
6. keep the call short and sell only the pilot  
7. follow up on all non-responses

### That is the whole job
Do not try to build a giant funnel model yet.  
Do not overthink CRM systems.  
At first, a spreadsheet is enough.

## Rules for you personally
Because you are not a trained salesperson, follow these rules:

### Rule 1
Do not “pitch the whole company.”  
Only pitch the pilot.

### Rule 2
Do not talk too much.  
Ask short questions and pause.

### Rule 3
Do not hide that you are early.  
Being early is okay if the pilot is small and measurable.

### Rule 4
Do not chase giant brands first.  
Get easy wins.

### Rule 5
Do not wait for perfect materials.  
Start outreach with this doc.

---

## The real goal of the first 10 conversations
It is **not** to close a huge deal.

It is to learn:
- which language gets interest
- which categories respond
- which objections come up most
- what pilot size feels acceptable
- whether brands understand the value quickly

That learning is your first win.
